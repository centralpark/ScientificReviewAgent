"Recent studies published over the last three years highlight a pivotal shift in oncology toward understanding, detecting, and targeting pre-cancerous (or preneoplastic) lesions. Intercepting cancer at these early stages is increasingly viewed as the most effective strategy for cancer prevention. The recent research spans molecular mapping, epidemiological modeling, novel diagnostic imaging, and chemoprevention. \n\nHere is a summary of the key focus areas from the recent literature:\n\n### 1. PreCancer Atlases and Molecular Mapping\nA major overarching theme is the development of "PreCancer Atlases,
    " largely driven by the National Cancer Institute\"s Human Tumor Atlas Network (HTAN). These atlases aim to map the cellular, spatial, and molecular topographies of precancerous lesions as they transition to invasive malignancies [1, 3, 4]. \n*   **Spatial and Single-Cell Profiling:** Researchers are using advanced tissue profiling to decode tumor progression and microenvironmental changes in colon, breast, lung, stomach, skin, and cervical precancers [3, 4].\n*   **Ovarian Precancer Subtypes:** One study performed an integrated spatial multiomics analysis on 166 ovarian precancerous lesions. It identified four distinct transcriptomic subtypes (proliferative, immunoreactive, dormant, and mixed). Notably, the immunoreactive subtype was linked to germline *BRCA1/2* mutations and exhibited a double "two-hit" mechanism involving *TP53* and *BRCA1/2* [2].\n*   **The Hallmarks of Precancer:** Defining the physiological "hallmarks" of early lesions—such as genetic and epigenetic aging—is recognized as crucial for developing accurate risk stratification models that prevent patient overtreatment while effectively intercepting malignancies [5].\n\n### 2. Modeling the Interception of Invasive Cancer\nMultiple studies underscore the massive public health impact of intercepting preneoplastic lesions (PNL) before they progress to invasive cancer. \n*   Predictive modeling indicates that diagnosing and halting the progression of PNLs could drastically reduce the incidence of various cancers, with the highest potential impact projected for pancreas, stomach, bladder, prostate, breast, colorectal, and skin cancers [6, 9]. \n*   For example, modeling suggests that if 95% of pancreatic intraepithelial neoplasia lesions were successfully detected and intercepted, over 1,200 invasive pancreatic cancers could be avoided annually in the US population alone [9].\n\n### 3. Novel Diagnostic Technologies\nTo improve early detection, researchers are exploring non-invasive, high-resolution imaging technologies capable of identifying the functional hallmarks of pre-cancers. \n*   A recent study utilized label-free, two-photon excited fluorescence (TPEF) imaging to assess functional metabolic heterogeneity in human cervical epithelial biopsies. By measuring the cell\"s redox state and mitochondrial organization, the algorithm distinguished high-grade squamous intra-epithelial lesions (HSILs) from non-HSILs with high diagnostic accuracy (>90% sensitivity and 70% specificity). This corroborates that high-grade pre-cancers have a distinct metabolic profile driven by enhanced glycolysis and fatty acid oxidation [7].\n\n### 4. Health Disparities and Comorbidities\nResearch has also shed light on how systemic factors and comorbidities influence precancer progression across different demographics. \n*   An analysis of cervical cancer risk evaluated the relationship between chronic metabolic conditions and high-risk cervical intraepithelial neoplasia (hrCIN). It revealed a significant racial disparity: Black women who concurrently suffered from hypertension and hypercholesterolemia had nearly triple the risk of developing hrCIN compared to those without these comorbidities. This is likely due to systemic inflammation promoting HPV persistence and progression to precancerous lesions [8].\n\n### 5. Chemoprevention Trials\nClinical trials are actively testing pharmacological interventions to reverse or halt precancerous lesions. \n*   A phase IIa randomized, double-blind clinical trial investigated the use of the drug eflornithine in patients with gastric premalignant conditions (such as gastric atrophy and intestinal metaplasia) in high-risk regions like Honduras and Puerto Rico. While the 18-month treatment did not result in significant changes to histology scores, eflornithine was safe, well-tolerated, and significantly reduced long-term DNA damage, supporting its potential role in future chemoprevention strategies [10].\n\n***\n\n### References\n[1] https://doi.org/10.1158/1940-6207.capr-22-0435\n[2] https://doi.org/10.1158/0008-5472.can-25-3189\n[3] https://doi.org/10.1158/2159-8290.cd-nb2022-0041\n[4] https://doi.org/10.1158/1940-6207.capr-22-0473\n[5] https://doi.org/10.1158/2159-8290.cd-23-1550\n[6] https://doi.org/10.1158/1055-9965.epi-25-0609\n[7] https://doi.org/10.1158/1538-7445.genfunc25-a009\n[8] https://doi.org/10.1158/1538-7755.disp22-c098\n[9] https://doi.org/10.1158/1538-7445.am2024-3447\n[10] https://doi.org/10.1158/1940-6207.capr-25-0364"
